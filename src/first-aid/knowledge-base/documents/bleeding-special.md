# Bleeding — Special Populations (Children, Elderly, Anticoagulant Users)

**Source: American Red Cross; American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP); Mayo Clinic**

## Children
- Apply gentle but firm pressure; children panic easily — speak calmly and reassure throughout.
- Children have smaller blood volumes; even moderate blood loss can cause shock faster than in adults. Watch for pallor and limpness.
- Do not use tourniquets on infants under 1 year; apply firm direct pressure only.
- For nosebleeds: sit child upright, lean slightly forward, pinch the soft part of the nose for 10 minutes continuously. Do not tilt head back.
- Seek emergency care sooner than you would for adults if bleeding does not slow within 5 minutes.

## Elderly
- Skin is thinner and more fragile; wounds may appear worse than they are but take longer to stop bleeding.
- Higher likelihood of blood thinners (warfarin, aspirin, clopidogrel, newer anticoagulants). These drugs prevent normal clotting — expect significantly prolonged bleeding.
- Apply pressure longer than usual (15–20 minutes minimum) and do not be alarmed if it takes more time.
- Even minor falls causing head wounds require medical evaluation in elderly patients.

## Anticoagulant / Blood Thinner Users
- Standard direct pressure still works but requires extended application time (15–30 minutes).
- Do not apply a tourniquet as a first measure; exhaust direct pressure first.
- These patients almost always require hospital evaluation for any wound beyond a minor scrape.
- Inform emergency responders of all medications the person is taking.

## Internal Bleeding (All Ages)
Suspect if person was struck, fell, shows shock signs without visible wound, has a rigid or distended abdomen, coughs or vomits blood, or has blood in urine.
Do not give food, water, or pain medication. Call emergency services immediately and keep the person still and warm.
