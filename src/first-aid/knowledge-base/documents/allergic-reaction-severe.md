# Allergic Reaction — Severe (Anaphylaxis)

**Source: World Allergy Organization (WAO); American Red Cross; AAAAI; WHO Emergency Care**

## Applicable Situations
Anaphylaxis: rapid systemic allergic reaction with throat/tongue swelling, severe breathing difficulty, drop in blood pressure, loss of consciousness. Life-threatening within minutes.

## Step-by-Step First Aid

1. Call emergency services immediately — anaphylaxis is life-threatening and progresses rapidly.
2. Use epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen) if available and prescribed. Inject into the outer mid-thigh (can be through clothing). Hold for 10 seconds. Note the time.
3. After epinephrine, lay the person flat with legs elevated unless breathing is easier sitting upright — never let an anaphylaxis patient stand.
4. If the person loses consciousness and stops breathing normally, begin CPR.
5. If a second epinephrine dose is available and symptoms return or do not improve after 5–15 minutes, administer a second dose.
6. Antihistamines and steroids are secondary treatments and do NOT replace epinephrine — do not rely on antihistamines alone for anaphylaxis.
7. Remove any remaining allergen (bee stinger: scrape with fingernail or card, do not squeeze).
8. Keep the person still and warm. Do not give oral food or liquids.
9. Even if symptoms improve after epinephrine, hospital evaluation is mandatory — biphasic anaphylaxis can recur hours later.
10. Monitor airway, breathing, and consciousness until emergency services arrive.

## Epinephrine Is the Only Effective Treatment
Antihistamines take 30–60 minutes to act. Epinephrine works within minutes. Never withhold epinephrine due to uncertainty.

## Biphasic Anaphylaxis
Symptoms can return 4–12 hours later even without re-exposure. Hospital observation for minimum 4–8 hours is required.
