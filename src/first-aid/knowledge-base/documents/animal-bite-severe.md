# Animal Bite — Severe (Deep Wound, Wild Animal, High Rabies Risk, Snake Bite)

**Source: WHO Rabies Post-Exposure Prophylaxis Guidelines; American Red Cross; CDC; WHO Snake Bite Guidelines**

## Applicable Situations
Deep puncture or laceration from any animal, bite from a wild animal or unvaccinated animal, any bat contact, or snakebite.

## Step-by-Step First Aid

1. Call emergency services or go to the nearest hospital immediately. Do not delay — rabies post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) must start as soon as possible.
2. Wash the wound immediately under running water for a minimum of 15 minutes. Thorough washing is the most critical first-aid step for rabies prevention.
3. Apply antiseptic (povidone-iodine or 70% alcohol) after washing.
4. Control significant bleeding with direct pressure.
5. Do not suture or close the wound in the field.

## Snakebite First Aid
- Keep the person calm and completely still. Movement accelerates venom spread through the lymphatic system.
- Immobilise the bitten limb in a position below heart level.
- Remove all jewellery, watches, and tight clothing from the bitten limb before swelling begins.
- Do not cut the wound, suck out venom, apply a tourniquet, apply ice, or give aspirin/ibuprofen — all are ineffective and cause additional harm.
- Go to hospital immediately. Antivenom is the only effective treatment for envenomation.
- If possible, photograph the snake from a safe distance for identification — do not approach or handle the snake.

## Rabies PEP Decision
Rabies is almost universally fatal once symptoms develop but is 100% preventable with timely post-exposure prophylaxis. Seek PEP for: bat contact (even without visible bite), wild animal bite, bite from an unvaccinated animal, or bite from an animal unavailable for observation.
