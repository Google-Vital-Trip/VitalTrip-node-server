# Heatstroke First Aid

## Overview
Heatstroke is a life-threatening condition where body temperature rises above 40°C (104°F) and the body's cooling mechanism fails. It is a medical emergency requiring immediate cooling and emergency services.

## Heat-Related Illness Spectrum

### Heat Cramps
- Muscle cramps during exercise in heat
- Move to cool area, rest, hydrate with water or sports drink

### Heat Exhaustion
- Heavy sweating, weakness, cold/pale/clammy skin
- Weak pulse, nausea, possible fainting
- Core temperature normal or slightly elevated (<40°C)

### Heatstroke (EMERGENCY)
- Core temperature >40°C (104°F)
- Hot, red skin — may be dry (classic) or sweaty (exertional)
- Rapid strong pulse
- Confusion, altered consciousness, may be unconscious
- NOT sweating (classic heatstroke)

## Step-by-Step First Aid

1. **Call emergency services immediately.** Heatstroke is fatal without rapid cooling.

2. **Move to a cool environment.** Get the person into shade, air conditioning, or cool building immediately.

3. **Remove excess clothing.** Remove outer clothing and anything restrictive.

4. **Cool rapidly using any available method:**
   - Immerse in cool (not ice cold) water if possible
   - Apply ice packs to neck, armpits, and groin (major blood vessels)
   - Spray with cool water and fan vigorously
   - Apply cool wet cloths over entire body
   - The goal: reduce temperature to 38–39°C as fast as possible

5. **Do not use ice water immersion for elderly or those with heart conditions.** Use cool water and evaporative cooling instead.

6. **Fan the person continuously.** Fanning dramatically increases evaporative cooling.

7. **Monitor body temperature.** If thermometer available, aim for 38–39°C. Stop aggressive cooling at that point to avoid overcooling.

8. **Hydrate if conscious and able to swallow.** Cool water in small sips. No alcohol. No caffeine.

9. **Do not give fever-reducing medication (aspirin, ibuprofen).** These do not work for heatstroke and may worsen complications.

10. **Recovery position if unconscious.** Roll to side to protect airway. Be prepared for CPR if breathing stops.

## Heat Exhaustion vs Heatstroke — Key Difference
| | Heat Exhaustion | Heatstroke |
|---|---|---|
| Skin | Cold, clammy | Hot, red |
| Sweating | Heavy | May be absent |
| Mental status | Normal/dizzy | Confused/unconscious |
| Temperature | Normal to <40°C | >40°C |
| Action | Cool, hydrate | Emergency services |

## Prevention
- Drink water before, during, and after physical activity
- Avoid peak heat hours (10am–4pm)
- Wear light, loose clothing
- Acclimatize gradually to hot environments
- Never leave children or pets in parked cars
