# Animal Bite — Minor (Superficial, Low Rabies Risk)

**Source: American Red Cross; CDC; WHO Rabies Guidelines; Mayo Clinic**

## Applicable Situations
Superficial puncture or scratch from a domestic pet (vaccinated dog or cat) with minor bleeding that stops quickly. Low-risk geographic area.

## Step-by-Step First Aid

1. Wash the wound immediately and thoroughly under running water for at least 5 minutes. Vigorous washing is the single most effective measure against rabies transmission and infection.
2. Clean with soap and water for an additional 2–3 minutes, working soap into all wound edges and puncture depths.
3. Apply an antiseptic solution (povidone-iodine, 70% alcohol) to the wound after washing.
4. Control any bleeding with gentle direct pressure using a clean cloth.
5. Cover with a clean bandage. For puncture wounds, cover loosely to allow drainage.
6. Do not close animal bite wounds with adhesive strips or sutures in the field — puncture wounds need to drain to prevent deep infection.
7. Seek medical evaluation within 24 hours even for minor bites. A healthcare provider must assess infection risk and tetanus status.
8. Report the bite to local animal control if the animal is unknown.
9. Observe the biting animal (if a known pet) for 10 days for any signs of illness — this is the standard protocol.

## Rabies Risk Assessment
Low risk: vaccinated domestic animal with known owner in a low-rabies region. Still requires medical assessment.
Higher risk: any wild animal (bats, raccoons, foxes, skunks), unvaccinated animal, or animal that cannot be observed for 10 days — treat as high risk.
