# Animal Bite First Aid

## Overview
Animal bites carry risks of bleeding, infection, and potentially rabies. Prompt and thorough wound cleaning is the most critical first-aid step. All animal bites require medical evaluation.

## Step-by-Step First Aid

1. **Ensure your own safety.** Do not approach the biting animal. If the animal is still aggressive, move away and call animal control.

2. **Call emergency services** for bites with severe bleeding, bites near eyes/face/neck/joints, bites that have crushed tissue, or any snake/spider bite with systemic symptoms.

3. **Control bleeding.** Apply direct pressure with clean cloth or gauze. Elevate the limb if possible.

4. **Clean the wound thoroughly.** This is the most important step for infection and rabies prevention. Wash with soap and running water for at least 5 minutes, using friction to scrub inside the wound.

5. **Flush with clean water.** Rinse the wound with a steady stream of clean water for several more minutes after washing with soap.

6. **Apply antiseptic.** Povidone-iodine or alcohol solution applied to the wound significantly reduces infection risk.

7. **Cover with sterile bandage.** Apply loosely — do not close a bite wound tightly (promotes infection). Seek medical care for suturing decision.

8. **Seek immediate medical care.** All but the most superficial animal bites require medical evaluation for:
   - Rabies post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP)
   - Tetanus prophylaxis
   - Antibiotic prescription
   - Wound closure decision

9. **Note information about the animal.** Species, behavior, vaccination status, whether it was provoked. Do not kill the animal unnecessarily — a living animal can be tested.

10. **Monitor for infection.** Signs: increasing redness, swelling, warmth, pus, red streaks from wound, fever. Seek medical care immediately if these appear.

## Rabies Risk Assessment
High-risk animals: dogs, cats, bats, raccoons, foxes, coyotes, skunks, monkeys
Low-risk animals: rodents (rats, mice, squirrels), rabbits, livestock (rarely transmit rabies)

- **Bat bites:** High risk even if bite not noticed — bats have small teeth. If you wake up with a bat in the room, assume exposure.
- **Unprovoked attack:** Higher rabies concern
- **Vaccinated domestic animal with known owner:** Lower risk, but still requires medical evaluation

## Snake Bites

1. Move away from the snake immediately
2. Immobilize the bitten limb below heart level
3. Remove rings, watches, tight clothing near bite
4. Note snake appearance (color, pattern) for antivenom selection
5. Do NOT: cut and suck wound, apply tourniquet, apply ice, use electric shock
6. Emergency services — antivenom administration requires hospital

## Signs of Envenomation (Snake)
- Severe pain, rapid swelling, discoloration at bite site
- Nausea, vomiting, dizziness
- Difficulty breathing, altered consciousness
- Neurotoxic signs: drooping eyelids, slurred speech, weakness

## Insect Stings
- Remove bee stinger by scraping (card edge) — do not squeeze
- Apply cold pack for pain/swelling
- Watch for allergic reaction signs for 30 minutes
- Epinephrine and emergency services if anaphylaxis develops
