# IFRC 2025 Guidelines - RAG Extraction Notes

## How this source should be used in the service

Use this source as the main global first-aid reference for layperson-facing guidance. It is best suited for answers that need practical first-aid actions, safety checks, escalation to emergency services, and user-friendly explanation. Do not use it as a replacement for local emergency protocols, medical diagnosis, or clinician-level treatment.

Recommended retrieval weight: high for general first-aid, bystander support, CPR/AED, choking, bleeding, burns, bites/stings, poisoning, stroke, chest pain, anaphylaxis, seizures, temperature-related illness, and psychological first aid.

## Global answer policy derived from this source

- Start with scene safety and personal safety.
- Encourage emergency medical services when symptoms are severe, life-threatening, rapidly worsening, or uncertain.
- Give simple, stepwise actions that a bystander can perform.
- Avoid advanced procedures unless the user is trained and the local context permits it.
- Adapt guidance to local emergency numbers, available resources, cultural context, and user capability.
- Keep the person warm, monitored, and reassured while waiting for help.
- For unconscious or abnormal breathing cases, prioritize emergency activation, CPR/AED when appropriate, and dispatcher instructions.

## General approach

The first response should be structured around safety, recognition, call for help, immediate care, monitoring, and handover. A safe answer should first ask whether the scene is safe, whether the person is responsive and breathing normally, and whether emergency services have been contacted when needed. For a global service, map the emergency call instruction to the detected country or location.

## Unresponsive person

For an unresponsive person, the service should separate two branches: breathing normally versus not breathing normally. If breathing is normal, maintain an open airway, place the person in a safe position if appropriate, monitor breathing, and call emergency services. If breathing is absent or abnormal, instruct the user to call emergency services, start CPR if trained or instructed, use an AED if available, and follow dispatcher guidance.

## Choking

For choking, distinguish mild airway obstruction from severe obstruction. If the person can cough, breathe, or speak, encourage coughing and observe closely. If the person cannot breathe, cough effectively, speak, or becomes cyanotic or unresponsive, activate emergency help and use standard choking first-aid techniques appropriate to age and training. For infants and children, avoid simply copying adult actions; age-specific instructions are required.

## Severe bleeding

For severe external bleeding, prioritize direct pressure, emergency activation, and continuous monitoring. The answer should guide the bystander to expose the bleeding site if safe, apply firm pressure with a clean cloth or dressing, avoid repeatedly removing soaked dressings, and keep the person still. Escalate urgently for spurting blood, uncontrolled bleeding, signs of shock, major trauma, amputation, chest/abdomen penetration, or bleeding that does not slow with pressure.

## Burns

For burns, retrieve guidance that prioritizes stopping the burning process, cooling the burn with clean cool running water when available, removing constricting items if not stuck, covering with a clean non-stick dressing, and seeking medical care for serious burns. Avoid recommending ice, butter, oils, adhesive dressings over the burn, or breaking blisters. Escalate for face, airway, hands, genitals, large/deep burns, chemical/electrical burns, children, older adults, or breathing difficulty after smoke exposure.

## Chest pain, stroke, anaphylaxis, seizure

Chest pain should trigger emergency escalation, rest, monitoring, and local protocol-aware medication guidance only when safe and permitted. Stroke answers should use simple recognition cues such as face, arm, speech, and time-based urgency, and should avoid delay. Anaphylaxis answers should emphasize emergency activation, epinephrine auto-injector use if prescribed/available and the user knows how, positioning, and monitoring. Seizure answers should protect from injury, time the seizure, avoid putting objects in the mouth, avoid restraining movements, and call emergency help for prolonged, repeated, first-time, pregnancy-related, water-related, trauma-related, or breathing-related concerns.

## Bites, stings, poisoning

For animal bites, clean and cover wounds and escalate for deep wounds, infection risk, rabies/tetanus concerns, or venomous exposure. For snakebite, keep the person still, remove constricting items, avoid cutting/sucking/ice/tourniquet-style folk remedies, and seek urgent care. For poisoning, identify the substance if safe, call poison control/emergency services, avoid inducing vomiting unless directed by professionals, and preserve containers or labels for handover.

## Temperature and environmental illness

For heat illness, move to a cooler place, reduce heat exposure, cool the person, give fluids only if awake and able to swallow, and escalate for confusion, collapse, seizure, very high temperature, or worsening symptoms. For hypothermia and frostbite, move to shelter, remove wet clothing, warm gradually, protect affected parts, and seek urgent care for severe exposure or altered mental status. For altitude or decompression illness, remove from exposure when safe, limit exertion, and seek specialized care urgently when severe symptoms appear.

## Psychological first aid and crisis content

Psychological first-aid answers should focus on safety, calm presence, listening, practical support, connection to trusted people/services, and avoiding pressure to talk. For suicidal ideation, the service must not rely on generic reassurance; it should encourage immediate local crisis support, emergency services when there is imminent risk, and staying with or contacting trusted support where safe.
