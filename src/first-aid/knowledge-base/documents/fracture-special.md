# Fracture — Special Populations (Children, Elderly, Growth Plate, Rib)

**Source: American Academy of Pediatrics; Mayo Clinic; American Red Cross**

## Children — Growth Plate Fractures
- Children's bones are softer and more flexible; they may have significant fractures with minimal visible deformity (greenstick fracture).
- Growth plate (physis) injuries at the ends of long bones are serious in children — improper treatment can cause permanent growth disruption.
- If a child is unwilling to move or bear weight on a limb after trauma, assume fracture until proven otherwise by X-ray.
- Do not apply rigid splints without padding; children's circulation is sensitive to pressure.
- Always seek medical evaluation — do not treat as a minor sprain in children under 12.

## Elderly — Fragility Fractures
- Osteoporosis makes bones fragile; fractures can occur from minimal trauma (stepping off a kerb, a simple fall).
- Hip fractures are most dangerous: mortality rate of 20–30% within one year in elderly adults. Do not attempt to move a person with suspected hip fracture — call emergency services.
- Wrist and vertebral compression fractures are common. Suspect spinal fracture in any elderly patient with back pain after a fall.
- Even if the person says they can walk, have them evaluated — an undisplaced hip fracture can still allow limited weight bearing initially.

## Rib Fractures
- Do not strap or tightly bandage the chest — this restricts breathing and can cause pneumonia.
- Encourage normal breathing despite pain; shallow breathing leads to lung complications.
- Seek medical care for multiple rib fractures or any rib fracture with breathing difficulty.
- A flail chest (section of ribs moving opposite to normal breathing motion) is a life-threatening emergency — call for help immediately.
