# Hypothermia — Special Situations (Immersion/Drowning, Children, Elderly, Avalanche)

**Source: Wilderness Medical Society; American Red Cross; American Academy of Pediatrics**

## Cold Water Immersion
- Cold water extracts heat 25 times faster than cold air at the same temperature.
- Remove the person from the water as quickly as possible — horizontal extraction if possible to avoid post-rescue collapse (standing causes blood to pool in legs, triggering cardiac arrest).
- Do not allow the person to climb out of the water themselves if hypothermic — assist them to remain horizontal during rescue.
- Remove wet clothing, insulate immediately, call emergency services.
- Drowning: if the person is not breathing, begin CPR regardless of how cold or stiff they appear.

## Children
- Children develop hypothermia faster than adults due to higher surface-area-to-body-mass ratio and thinner fat insulation.
- Infants cannot shiver — hypothermia can develop silently. Signs: cold skin, decreased movement, weak cry, poor feeding.
- All hypothermia in children under 1 year requires emergency care immediately.
- Rewarm with skin-to-skin contact (caregiver holding infant against bare chest) and warm insulation.

## Elderly
- Reduced metabolic rate and impaired thermoregulation mean elderly people can develop hypothermia even indoors at mild temperatures.
- Indoor hypothermia is common in winter in unheated homes.
- Many cardiac and sedative medications impair the shivering response.
- Even mild hypothermia (35°C) in elderly patients requires medical evaluation.

## Avalanche Burial
- Buried survivors can survive up to 35 minutes in an air pocket; survival drops sharply after.
- Once extracted: assess airway first (clear packed snow from mouth), then treat for hypothermia and traumatic injuries simultaneously.
- All avalanche victims with cardiac arrest should receive CPR and be transported for hospital rewarming.
