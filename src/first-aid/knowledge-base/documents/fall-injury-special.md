# Fall Injury — Special Populations (Elderly, Children, Anticoagulants, Head Injury Monitoring)

**Source: American Geriatrics Society; American Academy of Pediatrics; Mayo Clinic; CDC**

## Elderly Falls
- Hip fractures occur in up to 1% of elderly people annually; fall-related hip fracture has 20–30% one-year mortality.
- Do not help an elderly person up before excluding hip fracture: ask them to move both legs before standing. If they cannot lift either leg, suspect hip fracture.
- If hip fracture is suspected: do not attempt to move the person. Call emergency services. Keep them warm and still. Comfort and reassure.
- Head injuries in elderly patients: the brain shrinks with age, creating space for slow bleeds (subdural haematoma) that may not cause symptoms for days to weeks after a fall. Even a "minor" head bump requires medical evaluation.
- Elderly patients on blood thinners (warfarin, newer anticoagulants): any head impact requires immediate emergency evaluation — bleeding risk is dramatically elevated.

## Children
- Children's bones are more flexible; a child who cries after a fall and then seems fine may still have a fracture (toroidal/buckle fracture is common).
- Head injury red flags in children: loss of consciousness however brief, repeated vomiting (more than once), seizure, increasing drowsiness, bulging fontanelle in infants.
- Any child under 2 years with a head injury should be medically evaluated even if asymptomatic.

## Head Injury Observation at Home (If Not Hospitalised)
Wake the person every 2 hours during the first night to check they can be aroused and respond normally.
Seek emergency care immediately if: increasing headache, repeated vomiting, confusion worsening, weakness developing, seizure, or difficulty waking.
