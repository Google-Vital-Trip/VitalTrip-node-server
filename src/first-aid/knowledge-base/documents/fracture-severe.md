# Fracture — Severe (Open Fracture, Femur, Pelvis, Spinal Suspected)

**Source: American Red Cross; WHO Emergency Surgical Care; AHA**

## Applicable Situations
Bone protruding through skin (open/compound fracture), femur or pelvic fracture (suspected from high-energy trauma), or suspected spinal injury after fall/accident.

## Step-by-Step First Aid

1. Call emergency services immediately. Severe fractures carry risk of life-threatening blood loss and nerve damage.
2. Do not move the person unless there is immediate life-threatening danger. Moving incorrectly can cause permanent paralysis.
3. For open fractures: do not push bone back in. Cover the wound and exposed bone loosely with a clean, moist dressing. Control bleeding with gentle pressure around (not on) the bone.
4. For suspected spinal injury: keep the head, neck, and spine in a straight line. Use your hands to stabilise the head if necessary. Do not let the person twist or bend their neck.
5. Immobilise in position found. Do not straighten a limb that is deformed — splint it in its current position with adequate padding.
6. For femur fracture (mid-thigh): suspect major blood loss (up to 1–1.5 litres internally). Treat for shock: lay flat, keep warm, monitor vital signs.
7. For suspected pelvis fracture: do not apply a pelvic binder unless trained. Keep the person still and call for help.
8. Monitor breathing and consciousness every 5 minutes.
9. Keep the person warm to prevent shock-related hypothermia.

## Blood Loss Warning
Femur fracture: up to 1,500 mL blood loss. Pelvic fracture: up to 3,000 mL. Both are immediately life-threatening without definitive hospital care.
