# Fall Injury First Aid

## Overview
Falls can cause fractures, head injuries, and spinal injuries. Spinal injury assessment is the highest priority — moving a person with an unstable spine can cause permanent paralysis.

## Initial Assessment

### Mechanism of Injury — High-Risk Indicators for Spinal Injury
- Fall from height greater than standing height
- High-speed impact (motor vehicle accident, sports collision)
- Diving into shallow water
- Fall in elderly person
- Direct blow to head or neck

### Head Injury Severity
- **Minor:** Brief confusion, headache, no loss of consciousness
- **Moderate:** Loss of consciousness <30 minutes, post-traumatic amnesia
- **Severe:** Loss of consciousness >30 minutes, focal neurological deficits, skull fracture signs

## Step-by-Step First Aid

1. **Do not move the person** if spinal injury is suspected. Stabilize head and neck in the position found with your hands until emergency services arrive.

2. **Call emergency services** for falls from height, suspected spinal injury, loss of consciousness, head injury with confusion, or signs of major fracture.

3. **Check responsiveness, breathing, and pulse.** DRSABCD: Danger, Response, Send for help, Airway, Breathing, CPR if needed, Defibrillation.

4. **Control any bleeding.** Apply direct pressure to wounds without moving the head/spine.

5. **Assess for head injury.** Signs of serious head injury:
   - Loss of consciousness (even briefly)
   - Confusion, disorientation, memory loss
   - Severe headache
   - Repeated vomiting
   - Seizure
   - Clear fluid from nose or ears (cerebrospinal fluid leak — emergency)
   - Battle's sign (bruising behind ear) or raccoon eyes (bruising around eyes) — delayed signs of skull fracture

6. **Assess for spinal injury.** Ask about neck/back pain, numbness, tingling, or weakness in extremities. If any present, do not move.

7. **Keep the person still and reassured.** Tell them not to move their head or neck until assessed by emergency responders.

8. **If the person must be moved** (immediate danger), log roll as a unit keeping head, neck, and spine aligned — requires at least 3 people with one controlling the head.

9. **Apply ice to suspected fractures or sprains.** Wrap ice in cloth to reduce swelling.

10. **Monitor for deterioration.** Watch for worsening confusion, new neurological symptoms, or vital sign changes.

## Signs Requiring Immediate Emergency Care
- Any loss of consciousness, even brief
- Confusion, combativeness, or altered mental status
- Weakness or paralysis in any limb
- Neck or back pain after impact
- Visual disturbances, unequal pupils
- Repeated vomiting (>2 episodes)
- Seizure following injury
- Clear fluid from ears or nose
- Child <2 years with any head injury

## Elderly Falls
- Higher fracture risk, especially hip fractures
- Hip fracture: severe groin/thigh pain, leg rotated outward, shortened compared to other leg
- Do not attempt to stand them up — call emergency services
- High mortality risk if hip fracture not treated promptly

## Wrist Fracture (Colles Fracture — Common Fall Injury)
- Deformity at wrist, severe pain
- Splint with wrist in neutral position
- Apply ice
- Medical evaluation required for proper alignment and casting

## Ankle Sprain vs Fracture
- Ottawa Ankle Rules: X-ray needed if unable to bear weight OR tenderness at specific bony points
- Apply RICE: Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation
- If unable to walk, fracture possible — immobilize and seek care
