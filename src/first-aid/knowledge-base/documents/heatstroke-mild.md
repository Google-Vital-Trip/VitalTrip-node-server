# Heat Illness — Heat Exhaustion (Mild-Moderate)

**Source: American Red Cross; Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC); Mayo Clinic**

## Applicable Situations
Heat exhaustion: heavy sweating, cool/pale/clammy skin, fast and weak pulse, nausea, headache, dizziness, weakness. Core temperature below 40°C (104°F). Person is still conscious and coherent.

## Step-by-Step First Aid

1. Move the person immediately to a cool environment: air-conditioned building, shaded area, or in front of a fan. Get out of direct sun.
2. Lay the person down and elevate their legs 20–30 cm to improve blood flow to the brain.
3. Remove excess clothing, belts, and tight items to allow heat to escape.
4. Cool the person actively: apply cool, wet cloths to the skin — focus on neck, armpits, and groin (areas with large blood vessels). Fan while applying wet cloths for maximum evaporative cooling.
5. If the person is conscious and can swallow without difficulty, give cool water or an oral rehydration solution. Give small sips — do not force large amounts quickly. Sports drinks with electrolytes are helpful.
6. Do not give caffeinated or alcoholic beverages.
7. Continue cooling and monitor core temperature if a thermometer is available; aim to reduce temperature.
8. Monitor consciousness level. If the person becomes confused, stops sweating (skin becomes hot and dry), or loses consciousness, treat as heatstroke and call emergency services immediately.
9. Most heat exhaustion cases resolve within 30 minutes of cooling and rehydration. If not improving, seek medical evaluation.

## Difference from Heatstroke
Heat exhaustion: sweating present, person coherent, temperature under 40°C.
Heatstroke: no sweating OR altered mental state, temperature over 40°C — call emergency services.
