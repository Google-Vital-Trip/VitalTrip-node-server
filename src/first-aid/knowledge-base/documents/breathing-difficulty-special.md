# Breathing Difficulty — Special Situations (Children, Croup, Anaphylaxis Airway, Drowning)

**Source: American Academy of Pediatrics; American Red Cross; AHA**

## Children — Croup
- Barking cough, stridor (high-pitched sound on breathing in), hoarse voice in children 6 months to 3 years, typically worse at night.
- Mild croup: take the child into cool night air or a steamy bathroom for 10–15 minutes — cool mist can reduce airway swelling.
- Keep the child calm and upright; crying worsens airway narrowing.
- Call emergency services if: stridor at rest (not just when upset), severe breathing difficulty, blue lips, drooling, stiff neck, or not improving.
- Do not use steam from boiling water — risk of burns.

## Epiglottitis (Emergency)
- High fever, drooling, difficulty swallowing, child sitting in a characteristic "tripod" position.
- Do not examine the throat or attempt to look in the mouth — this can cause complete airway spasm.
- Call emergency services immediately. Keep the child calm and upright. Do not lay down.

## Anaphylaxis Airway Compromise
- Throat and tongue swelling from anaphylaxis can cause complete airway obstruction within minutes.
- Administer epinephrine immediately (first-line treatment).
- If epinephrine is unavailable and the airway is obstructing, call emergency services and keep the person upright.
- Do not give antihistamines alone — they cannot reverse airway swelling quickly enough.

## Near-Drowning / Water Aspiration
- Remove from water. If unresponsive and not breathing normally, begin CPR immediately.
- Do not perform abdominal thrusts to remove water — they are ineffective and delay CPR.
- All near-drowning victims require hospital evaluation even if they recover fully — secondary drowning (delayed pulmonary oedema) can occur hours later.
