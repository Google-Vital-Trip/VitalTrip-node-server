# ILCOR 2025 First Aid CoSTR - RAG Extraction Notes

## How this source should be used in the service

Use this source as the evidence layer, not the main user-facing manual. It is best for deciding whether a first-aid claim is evidence-backed, uncertain, provider-specific, or context-dependent. Answers to users should be simplified and cross-checked with IFRC/WHO-style practical guidance.

Recommended retrieval weight: high for evidence-sensitive topics such as oxygen, recovery position, anaphylaxis, choking, aspirin for chest pain, glucose for hypoglycemia, stroke recognition, sepsis recognition, bleeding control, tourniquets, hemostatic dressings, burn cooling, dental avulsion, spinal motion restriction, and concussion recognition.

## Evidence framework for metadata

ILCOR evaluates first-aid questions by considering the recipient, the provider, the treatment, and the setting. This is useful for RAG filtering because a recommendation may differ depending on whether the helper is an untrained bystander, trained first-aid provider, or healthcare professional; whether equipment is available; and whether the environment is urban, remote, low-resource, or high-resource.

## General principles

For evidence-based first-aid answers, the system should avoid absolute claims when evidence is limited. Prefer wording such as “if trained and available,” “according to local protocol,” “seek emergency medical help,” and “do not delay emergency care.” Separate layperson-safe advice from provider-level or equipment-dependent interventions.

## Medical emergencies evidence topics

Pulse oximetry and supplemental oxygen are equipment- and training-dependent. The app should not tell untrained users to administer oxygen unless they are trained and local guidance allows it.

Recovery position guidance should be applied only when it does not conflict with CPR needs or major trauma concerns. Always prioritize normal breathing assessment and emergency activation.

Anaphylaxis guidance should prioritize recognition of severe allergic reaction, emergency activation, and epinephrine auto-injector use when prescribed/available and the user is able to use it safely. A second dose is a context-dependent escalation when symptoms persist and guidance/product instructions allow.

Foreign-body airway obstruction should distinguish mild obstruction from severe obstruction. Severe choking requires urgent action and emergency escalation. Age and provider training matter.

Chest pain guidance may include aspirin only when local protocol and contraindication screening permit. The service should avoid blanket medication instructions for unknown users.

Hypoglycemia guidance should distinguish conscious/able-to-swallow from reduced-consciousness states. Give oral sugar only when the person can safely swallow. Escalate if consciousness is impaired or symptoms do not improve.

Stroke recognition should emphasize rapid identification and urgent emergency care. Do not suggest driving oneself or waiting for symptoms to resolve.

Sepsis recognition is difficult for first-aid providers; the service should emphasize danger signs such as severe illness, altered mental status, breathing difficulty, cold/clammy skin, persistent fever or low temperature, and urgent medical evaluation.

## Trauma emergencies evidence topics

Spinal motion restriction is context-sensitive. For untrained users, the safest approach is to minimize movement, support the person in the position found when safe, and wait for trained help unless there is immediate danger.

Bleeding control evidence supports direct manual pressure as a core first-aid intervention. Tourniquets and hemostatic dressings are training- and equipment-dependent and should be framed carefully. For life-threatening limb bleeding where trained help or equipment is available, tourniquets may be relevant; otherwise direct pressure and emergency activation remain the user-safe baseline.

Burn cooling should be treated as a time-sensitive first-aid action, while avoiding harmful folk remedies. The system should recommend cooling with clean cool running water when available and escalating for serious burns.

Dental avulsion guidance should preserve the tooth and seek urgent dental care, but user-facing instructions must be simple and age-safe.

Concussion and head injury guidance should prioritize red flags: loss of consciousness, worsening headache, repeated vomiting, confusion, seizure, neck pain, neurological deficits, anticoagulant use, high-risk mechanism, or symptoms in children/older adults.

## Maternal, opioid, and special topics

Postpartum hemorrhage, opioid overdose, and supplemental oxygen topics may involve interventions that are appropriate only for trained responders or specific settings. For general users, the app should emphasize urgent emergency activation, local emergency number, dispatcher guidance, and basic safety/monitoring rather than advanced treatment details.

## RAG answer guardrails

- Never present evidence summaries as personalized diagnosis.
- Do not overstate low-certainty evidence.
- Mention provider-level requirements for interventions such as oxygen, tourniquet, hemostatic dressing, naloxone, and medication use.
- When evidence and user capability conflict, prioritize safe layperson actions.
- Combine this source with IFRC for practical first-aid wording and WHO/ICRC for structured assessment.
