# WHO/ICRC Basic Emergency Care - RAG Extraction Notes

## How this source should be used in the service

Use this source to structure emergency reasoning: initial assessment, danger recognition, ABCDE, SAMPLE history, trauma, breathing difficulty, shock, and altered mental status. It is more clinical than a layperson first-aid manual, so the service should convert retrieved content into safe, simple user actions unless the user is a trained provider.

Recommended retrieval weight: high for triage flow, danger signs, systematic assessment, severe injury, breathing difficulty, shock, altered consciousness, and handover summaries.

## Global answer policy derived from this source

- Use a systematic approach instead of jumping to a diagnosis.
- Check hazards and personal safety before touching the person.
- Assess and act in order: Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, Exposure.
- Treat immediately life-threatening problems as they are found.
- Use SAMPLE history when the person or bystanders can answer.
- Prepare a concise handover: situation, background, assessment, action taken, and response.
- Avoid giving advanced clinical instructions to untrained users.

## ABCDE assessment for RAG retrieval

Airway: Decide whether the airway is open, obstructed, or at risk. If airway obstruction is suspected, prioritize opening the airway, clearing visible obstruction only when safe, positioning, and urgent help.

Breathing: Look for abnormal breathing, severe shortness of breath, chest injury signs, cyanosis, noisy breathing, or exhaustion. Encourage emergency activation for severe or worsening breathing difficulty.

Circulation: Look for severe bleeding, weak/rapid pulse signs, cold/clammy skin, delayed capillary refill signs, collapse, or shock. Control major bleeding and call emergency services.

Disability: Check level of consciousness, seizure activity, confusion, weakness, severe headache, glucose-related symptoms if relevant, and signs of stroke or head injury.

Exposure: Look for hidden injuries, rash, burns, temperature exposure, or environmental causes while preserving dignity and preventing heat loss.

## SAMPLE history extraction

Use SAMPLE when the person is stable enough or bystanders can answer:

- Signs and symptoms
- Allergies
- Medications
- Past medical history or pregnancy
- Last oral intake or last known well time
- Events leading to the condition

For a travel emergency app, store SAMPLE responses in a handover-ready format and translate them if needed for local responders.

## Trauma approach

Trauma retrieval should emphasize safety, mechanism of injury, major bleeding, airway/breathing compromise, spinal risk, chest/abdominal injury, fractures, burns, and shock. For layperson answers, give low-risk actions: call emergency services, control bleeding, avoid unnecessary movement, keep warm, monitor breathing, and prepare location details.

## Difficulty in breathing

Breathing difficulty can come from asthma, allergic reaction, infection, drowning, chest injury, heart problems, or airway obstruction. User-facing guidance should not try to diagnose from minimal information. Instead, assess severity: can the person speak, are lips/face blue, is breathing noisy or very fast/slow, is there chest pain, altered mental status, severe allergy, or recent drowning/trauma. Severe signs should trigger emergency help.

## Shock

Shock should be treated as a life-threatening state. Retrieval should focus on recognizing collapse, pale/cold/clammy skin, confusion, weakness, severe bleeding, dehydration, infection, allergic reaction, trauma, or chest pain. User-facing guidance should prioritize emergency services, bleeding control, lying the person down if appropriate, warmth, no unnecessary food/drink, and monitoring.

## Altered mental status

Altered mental status includes confusion, agitation, fainting, seizure, poisoning, stroke, low glucose, infection, head injury, heat/cold exposure, or hypoxia. RAG answers should guide the user to check responsiveness and breathing, call emergency services for serious or unexplained changes, protect from harm, avoid giving anything by mouth if not fully alert, and gather SAMPLE information.

## Handover format for service output

Use a compact emergency handover block when the user needs to communicate with responders:

- Location and callback number
- Patient age/sex if known
- Main problem and time started
- Responsiveness and breathing status
- Major bleeding or visible injury
- Actions already taken
- Known allergies/medications/medical history
- Local language translation if available
