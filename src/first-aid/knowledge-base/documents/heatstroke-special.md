# Heat Illness — Special Populations (Children, Elderly, Athletes, Hot Car)

**Source: American Academy of Pediatrics; CDC; Mayo Clinic**

## Children
- Children overheat faster than adults and have less efficient sweat mechanisms; they are at high risk in hot weather.
- Children left in hot cars: car interiors can reach 54°C (130°F) within minutes on a warm day. This is life-threatening.
- If a child is found in a hot car: remove immediately, call emergency services, begin rapid cooling (cool water, fanning). Do not leave to get help first.
- Infants and toddlers cannot verbalise discomfort — watch for rapid breathing, flushed red skin, no tears when crying, and decreased alertness.
- For all childhood heatstroke cases, seek emergency care immediately even if the child appears to improve after cooling.

## Elderly
- Impaired thirst sensation means elderly people often do not drink enough fluid in hot weather.
- Reduced ability to sweat and impaired cardiovascular responses make them highly vulnerable.
- Many medications (diuretics, antihypertensives, anticholinergics) impair heat tolerance.
- Confusion in an elderly person in hot weather should be treated as heatstroke until proven otherwise.
- Ensure adequate hydration throughout the day, not only when thirsty.

## Athletes — Exertional Heatstroke
- Occurs in young, fit individuals during intense exercise in heat; skin may still be sweating (unlike classic heatstroke).
- Aggressive cooling is equally essential — cold water immersion is the gold standard.
- Do not delay cooling to transport the athlete. "Cool first, transport second."
- Rectal temperature is the only reliable measurement in exertional heatstroke; oral/axillary temperatures are inaccurate.
