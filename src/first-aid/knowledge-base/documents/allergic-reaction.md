# Allergic Reaction & Anaphylaxis First Aid

## Overview
Allergic reactions range from mild (hives, itching) to life-threatening anaphylaxis. Anaphylaxis requires epinephrine and emergency services immediately.

## Severity Levels

### Mild Allergic Reaction
- Hives, itching, redness
- Watery eyes, runny nose
- Localized swelling

### Severe Allergic Reaction (Anaphylaxis) — EMERGENCY
- Throat tightening or swelling, difficulty swallowing
- Difficulty breathing, wheezing, stridor
- Rapid or weak pulse
- Drop in blood pressure, dizziness, fainting
- Pale or bluish skin
- Nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps
- Confusion or loss of consciousness

## Step-by-Step First Aid

1. **Call emergency services immediately** if signs of anaphylaxis are present. Anaphylaxis can be fatal within minutes.

2. **Identify and remove the allergen.** Stop exposure — remove bee stinger by scraping (not squeezing), move away from stinging insect, stop eating the suspected food.

3. **Use epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen) if available.** Inject into the outer thigh (can inject through clothing). Hold for 10 seconds. Note the time. A second dose may be given after 5–15 minutes if symptoms persist.

4. **Call emergency services even after epinephrine.** Epinephrine is temporary — symptoms can return (biphasic reaction). Hospital observation for 4–8 hours is necessary.

5. **Position the person correctly:**
   - Breathing difficulty: sitting upright
   - Shock/faint: lying flat with legs elevated 30 cm
   - Unconscious: recovery position (on side)
   - Never force to stand or walk

6. **Administer antihistamine for mild reactions.** Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) for mild hives/itching. Note: antihistamines do NOT treat anaphylaxis and must not replace epinephrine.

7. **Monitor breathing and pulse.** Be prepared to perform CPR if the person stops breathing or loses pulse.

8. **Keep warm.** Cover with blanket to manage shock.

9. **Do not give oral medications if throat is swollen.** Risk of choking.

10. **Reassure the person.** Anxiety worsens symptoms. Stay calm and keep them calm.

## Common Allergen Triggers
- Foods: peanuts, tree nuts, shellfish, fish, milk, eggs, wheat, soy
- Insect stings: bees, wasps, fire ants
- Medications: penicillin, aspirin, NSAIDs
- Latex
- Exercise (exercise-induced anaphylaxis)

## After Epinephrine Administration
- Record time of injection
- Save the used injector for medical personnel
- Do not give antihistamine as substitute for epinephrine
- Remain still — exercise worsens anaphylaxis
