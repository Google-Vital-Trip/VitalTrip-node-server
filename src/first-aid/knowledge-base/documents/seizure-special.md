# Seizure — Special Populations (Children / Febrile Seizures, Pregnant Women, Diabetics)

**Source: American Academy of Pediatrics; Epilepsy Foundation; Mayo Clinic**

## Children — Febrile Seizures
- Febrile seizures occur in children aged 6 months to 5 years during rapid temperature rise; they are usually harmless.
- Do not panic. Place the child on the floor on their side. Protect from injury. Do not restrain.
- Do not put anything in the child's mouth. Do not immerse in cold water to lower fever during a seizure.
- Febrile seizures typically last under 3 minutes.
- Call emergency services if: seizure lasts over 5 minutes, child is under 6 months old, child does not fully recover, multiple seizures in 24 hours, or child has stiff neck or rash.
- After the seizure, give age-appropriate fever medication (paracetamol/ibuprofen) once fully conscious and able to swallow.
- A first febrile seizure always requires medical evaluation.

## Pregnant Women
- A seizure in a pregnant woman may indicate eclampsia (dangerous complication of pre-eclampsia) — call emergency services immediately.
- Turn the woman onto her left side to relieve pressure on the major blood vessels and improve blood flow to the fetus.
- Do not restrain. Protect from injury. Monitor the airway.
- Eclamptic seizures are a life-threatening emergency for both mother and baby; hospital care is essential.

## Diabetics — Hypoglycaemic Seizures
- Low blood sugar can trigger seizures in diabetic patients.
- Do not give anything by mouth during the seizure.
- Once fully conscious and able to swallow: give glucose gel inside the cheek, or 15–20 g fast-acting sugar (juice, glucose tablets).
- If the person does not fully recover, call emergency services.
- Inform emergency responders of the person's diabetic status.
