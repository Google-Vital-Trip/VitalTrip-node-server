# Burns — Special Situations (Children, Elderly, Sunburn, Chemical Eye Burns)

**Source: American Academy of Pediatrics; Mayo Clinic; American Burn Association**

## Children
- Children have thinner skin — burns that appear minor may be deep. Always seek medical evaluation for burns in children under 5.
- Cool with lukewarm water (not cold) to prevent hypothermia; children lose heat faster than adults.
- Do not use ice, cold water, or any creams. Do not break blisters.
- Burns larger than the child's palm, or any burn on face, hands, feet, or genitals, require emergency care.
- Children are at higher risk for infection and scarring — medical evaluation is recommended even for seemingly minor burns.

## Elderly
- Thinner, more fragile skin means deeper burns from shorter exposures; what seems minor may be severe.
- Reduced pain sensation means elderly patients may underreport burn severity.
- Pre-existing medical conditions complicate recovery — always seek medical care.

## Sunburn
- Mild: Cool compresses and cool baths; apply aloe vera gel or unscented moisturiser.
- Avoid further sun exposure. Stay hydrated. Take ibuprofen for pain and inflammation.
- Seek care if blistering, fever, chills, or severe pain develop (sun poisoning).
- Do not apply petroleum jelly or butter — traps heat.

## Chemical Burns to the Eye
- Immediately flush the eye with large amounts of clean water or saline for at least 20 minutes.
- Hold the eyelid open and allow water to flow from the inner corner outward.
- Remove contact lenses only if they come out easily without force.
- Do not rub the eye.
- Call emergency services and go to hospital immediately after flushing — all chemical eye exposures are serious.
