# Allergic Reaction — Special Situations (Children, Insect Stings, Food Allergy, Unknown Allergen)

**Source: American Academy of Pediatrics; AAAAI; American Red Cross; Mayo Clinic**

## Children and Anaphylaxis
- Children may not be able to describe throat tightness — watch for drooling, difficulty swallowing, hoarse cry, or refusing to eat.
- Epinephrine dosing is weight-based for children under 30 kg; use a junior EpiPen (0.15 mg) if available.
- Children with known severe allergies should always carry two doses of epinephrine.
- School-age children with known allergies: ask if they have their EpiPen immediately.

## Insect Sting Reactions
- Remove bee stinger by scraping sideways with a fingernail or credit card edge — do not squeeze the venom sac.
- For local reaction: ice, antihistamine, observe.
- Anaphylaxis from stings develops rapidly — administer epinephrine at first systemic signs.
- People who have had a previous systemic sting reaction have 60% risk of anaphylaxis on re-sting; they should always carry epinephrine.

## Food Allergy (Peanut, Tree Nut, Shellfish, Milk, Egg)
- Cross-contamination can trigger reactions — trace amounts are sufficient.
- Symptoms typically begin within 5–30 minutes of ingestion.
- Do not induce vomiting.
- If mild symptoms only (skin hives, sneezing): give antihistamine and monitor closely.
- Any systemic symptoms (throat, breathing, cardiovascular): epinephrine immediately.

## Unknown Allergen / First Reaction
- Treat based on symptoms, not known history.
- Mild skin-only reaction: antihistamine and observation.
- Any systemic involvement: treat as anaphylaxis with epinephrine.
- All first-time anaphylaxis episodes require hospital evaluation and allergy specialist referral.
