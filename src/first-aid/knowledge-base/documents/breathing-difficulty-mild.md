# Breathing Difficulty — Mild (Asthma Attack, Mild Shortness of Breath)

**Source: American Red Cross; Global Initiative for Asthma (GINA); Mayo Clinic**

## Applicable Situations
Mild to moderate shortness of breath, wheezing, mild chest tightness in a person who is still able to speak in full sentences. Known asthma or COPD with a rescue inhaler available.

## Step-by-Step First Aid

1. Keep calm and reassure the person — anxiety and panic worsen breathing difficulty by increasing oxygen demand.
2. Help the person sit upright or lean slightly forward with hands on knees or a table (tripod position). Do not lay them flat — this makes breathing harder.
3. Loosen any tight clothing around the neck and chest.
4. If the person has a prescribed rescue inhaler (salbutamol/albuterol blue inhaler): help them use it — 2–4 puffs with a spacer if available. Wait 20 minutes and repeat if still symptomatic.
5. Encourage slow, controlled breathing: breathe in through the nose for 4 counts, out through pursed lips for 6 counts. This reduces hyperventilation.
6. Move to fresh air away from any possible trigger (smoke, fumes, allergens, cold air).
7. Monitor the person closely — mild episodes can progress to severe.
8. If the person has a written asthma action plan, follow it.
9. After using the rescue inhaler twice with no improvement, or if symptoms are worsening, call emergency services.

## Signs of Worsening (Call Emergency Services)
- Cannot speak in full sentences
- Using neck and shoulder muscles to breathe
- Lips or fingernails turning blue
- Rescue inhaler has no effect
- Extreme fatigue from effort of breathing
