# Animal Bite — Special Situations (Marine Animals, Insects, Immunocompromised)

**Source: CDC; WHO; Mayo Clinic; Divers Alert Network (DAN)**

## Marine Animal Injuries

### Jellyfish Sting
- Remove the person from the water.
- Do not rub the affected area — this activates unfired nematocysts.
- Rinse with seawater (not fresh water — osmotic difference activates more stingers).
- Remove visible tentacles with tweezers or a card edge; do not use bare hands.
- Soak in hot water (as hot as tolerable, 40–45°C) for 20 minutes to denature venom proteins.
- For Portuguese Man O'War: similar steps; apply heat rather than vinegar.
- Call emergency services for facial/airway stings, anaphylaxis signs, or full-body involvement.

### Sea Urchin Spines
- Remove accessible spines with tweezers. Small deep spines can be left to dissolve.
- Soak in hot water for pain relief.
- Seek medical care if spines are near a joint, near the eye, or if infection develops.

### Stingray
- Immerse in hot water immediately (up to 45°C) for 30–90 minutes — heat denatures the venom and dramatically reduces pain.
- Go to hospital for wound cleaning (stingray wounds have a high infection risk).

## Immunocompromised Patients
- Patients on chemotherapy, immunosuppressants, or with HIV/diabetes are at extremely high risk of rapid severe infection from any animal bite.
- Even minor bites require immediate medical evaluation and likely prophylactic antibiotics.
- Pasteurella (dogs/cats), Capnocytophaga (dogs), and Streptococcus can cause life-threatening sepsis within hours in immunocompromised individuals.
